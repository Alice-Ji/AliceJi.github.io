Social Media Affordance Study

Problem: Platforms like Instagram shape user–creator relationships through affordances (e.g., comments), but it’s unclear how disabling them changes parasocial interaction and purchase intention of sponsored product.

Solution: Built a fully functional fake Instagram prototype in HTML/JS where I could toggle comments on/off. Participants scrolled and liked in a naturalistic environment with sponsored posts.

Methods: Logged user actions (comment length, frequency, sentiment) and paired with survey data.

Impact: Found that parasocial relationships only predicted purchase intention in the comment-enabled condition — when comments were disabled, PSR no longer translated into consumer action. Currently under conference peer review.

Shoppable Advertising Study

Problem: Brands are increasingly experimenting with shoppable ad formats, but it’s unclear whether consumers perceive them as engaging enhancements or intrusive disruptions — and whether platform context (social vs. streaming) changes their effectiveness.

Solution: Built interactive streaming TV and social media ad prototypes in HTML/JS, enabling side-by-side comparison of shoppable vs. non-shoppable experiences.

Methods: Captured interaction logs (clicks, overlay opens) and paired with survey measures of attention, attitudes, and purchase intent. Included open-ended responses on perceived intrusiveness.

Impact: Shoppable ads were rated as more surprising and intrusive, with lower purchase intent on social media. In contrast, on streaming TV, shoppable formats boosted purchase intent by creating a lean-forward commerce moment. Results highlight the importance of platform-fit when deploying interactive ad formats. Currently under journal peer review.

Gacha Behavioral Economies

Problem: Mobile games rely heavily on gacha mechanics to drive engagement and monetization, but it’s unclear how design choices (e.g., pull odds, banner framing, bonus gems) shape player spending and progression.

Solution: Built a fully functional gacha battle game in HTML/JS with summon, battle, and progression systems. The platform allows controlled manipulation of summon odds, banner design, animations, endowment bonuses, purchase pricing, and rarity effects.

Methods: Player behavior and spending patterns are logged via Apps Script, enabling experiments on how randomized rewards, completion bias, and perceived value drive engagement.

Impact: Provides a testbed for research questions in behavioral economics and game UX — e.g., how framing pull odds as advertising affects trust, or how bonus payments and rarity design shift purchase intention. Prototype demonstrates how gaming mechanics can reveal broader consumer decision-making patterns.

Social Media Pharmaceutical Advertising

Problem: Pharmaceutical advertisements are legally mandated to present both benefits and risks in their ads on social media, yet it’s unclear how consumer attention, emotional response, and memory are allocated to these distinct sections, potentially impacting critical health information recall.

Solution: Designed and executed an experimental study featuring realistic pharmaceutical ad stimuli embeded in simulated social media feed to examine participant engagement with benefit-focused (e.g., people frolicking) versus risk-focused (e.g., long scroll of side effects) content.

Methods: Employed advanced eye-tracking and facial emotion analysis to generate heat maps of visual attention, capture real-time emotional responses, and post-exposure memory recall assessments for both benefit and risk information.

Impact: Revealed that participants paid less attention to risk sections and displayed more eye wandering compared to the benefit section. This was corroborated by more negative facial emotions during risk exposure and poorer memory recall of risks compared to benefits. Presented at National Communication Association 2024 Annual Conference.
